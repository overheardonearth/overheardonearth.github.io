--- title: Financial Misdirection comments: true date: 2019-08-19 layout: post --- Consider the following clip. The guy on the right, Graham, is the interviewer; he has a personal finance channel on Youtube.
The interviewee exhibits his message masterfully, as though with a magician's misdirection. As for Graham, I understand he was trying to be friendly - he normally doesn't do interviews - but this clip was a bit irresponsible.

Basic investment considerations:

  • Liquidity: You can sell an ETF or stock in a fraction of a second during trading hours, with essentially no fees or negligible fees. This not the case for a vintage handbag.
  • Transparency: Furthermore, when you sell your ETF or stock, you can be reasonably certain that you've sold it for a fair price, because of the established exchanges and high volumes. The interviewee in fact emphasizes that there is no established exchange or reasonable volume for designer handbags, so quoted prices are spurious at best.
  • Volatility: The interviewee talks about the designer handbags as though they appreciate 14% every year, consistently. This is doubtful. I can't be any more precise than "doubtful," because of the previous point.
  • Price manipulation: The interviewee discusses this as though it's a good thing.
  • Diversification: Altria (America's primary tobacco company) also grew roughly 14% from 1980 to now, even despite its recent downturn. I doubt Graham would advise anyone of normal means to drop 100k into Altria stock.
I don't oppose buying a handbag if it aligns with your desires; I'd happily burn my money on a Steinway. I don't like that he pretends to have bought it as a wise investment.
